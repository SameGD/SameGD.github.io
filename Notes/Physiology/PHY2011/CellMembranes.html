Cells and Cell Membranes

Weeks 1 and 2

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the distribution of water through the different body compartments and gain a general knowledge of differences in ionic composition across the body fluids.
  • Learn the name and functions of the major cellular organelles, and the relative scale of their sizes.
  • Learn how cells are held together, especially the special junctions of tight junctions, desmosomes, and gap junctions linking cells together.
  • Know the major constituents of the mammalian cell membrane and their functions.
  • Learn the organization of the components as a cell membrane, how and why the cell membrane is a dynamic structure, and its roles.
  • Learn why the cell membrane is selectively permeable and the advantages of this property for the cell.
  • Learn the two general ways in which substances can cross the cell membrane.
  • Understand what is diffusion and that at equilibrium there is no NET movement, and know the factors that control diffusion rate.
  • Understand why lipid solubility is an important factor in controlling movement across the cell membrane.
  • Understand how water is able to traverse the cell membrane through Aquaporins.
  • Learn what is facilitated diffusion, the types of facilitated diffusion, and how it operates.
  • Understand why facilitated diffusion, like all forms of assisted transport, shows specificity, competition & saturation of transport rate.
  • Understand what is active transport and the energy requirements of active transport systems.
  • Learn the two trans-membrane protein systems that are used in active transport.
  • Learn that there are 3 classes of active transport systems.
  • Understand in detail the characteristics of one of these transport systems, Ion pumps and in particular the P-type ATPase ion pumps as exemplified by the Na+/K+ exchange pump and the muscle Ca++ pump.
  • Have a general awareness of the other two active transport systems, the ABC system and the Group translocation system.
  • Understand how active transport of ions can be coupled to the transport of other molecules, through a process called Secondary active transport and that secondary active transport is driven by ionic gradients.
  • Learn how to differentiate symport and antiport secondary active transport.
  • Understand Na+/Glucose co-transport in the intestine and how Glucose transport in this system is an example of Secondary active transport.
  • Learn and understand the properties of specificity, competition and saturation as general properties of all carrier-mediated systems.
  • Understand that osmosis is the movement of water down its concentration gradient and that it can occur because of differences in [H2O] in the presence of permeating or impermeant solutes.
  • Understand Osmotic Pressure, how it is measured in Osmolarity, and how Osmolarity depends on the number of active particles (and therefore the effective [ ]).
  • Understand the meaning of Iso-osmotic, Hypo-osmotic and Hyper-osmotic and learn that these compare the Osmotic pressure of two solutions.
  • Learn that animal cells have a limited capacity to accommodate changes in volume and that H2O flow can destroy cell function.
  • Appreciate why it is important to know if a solute is impermeant due to it’s effects on H2O flow into or out of the cell.
  • Learn that Tonicity tells us what will happen to a cell placed in a solution containing impermeant solutes.
  • Understand the conditions that define Isotonicity, Hypotonicity and Hypertonicity.
  • Learn the fate of Red Blood Cells placed in solutions that are Isotonic, or Hypotonic or Hypertonic to their internal contents.
  • Learn the differences between Osmolarity and Tonicity.
